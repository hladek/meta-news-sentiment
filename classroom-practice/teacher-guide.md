# Teacher Guide

## Learning Objective

Students will use a sentiment analysis tool to practice metacognition: planning, monitoring, evaluating, and revising their thinking while interpreting written text.

## Before Class

Prepare the Sentiment Analysis app so students can access it. Choose one or more short texts from [sample-texts.md](sample-texts.md), or provide your own text that contains mixed tone, ambiguous language, or sentence-level shifts in sentiment.

## Lesson Flow

### 1. Frame the Activity: 5 minutes

Tell students that the goal is not to prove that the AI is right. The goal is to observe their own thinking.

Use this framing:

> Today you will make predictions, record confidence, compare your reasoning with a tool, and revise your interpretation. The important part is the quality of your thinking process.

### 2. Individual Prediction: 8 minutes

Students read the selected text without using the tool.

They complete Part 1 of the [student worksheet](student-worksheet.md):

- Break the text into sentences.
- Predict each sentence's sentiment: positive, negative, or neutral.
- Rate confidence from 1 to 5.
- Write evidence from the sentence.

### 3. Tool Analysis: 7 minutes

Students paste the same text into the Sentiment Analysis app and run the analysis.

They copy the tool's sentence-level labels into Part 2 of the worksheet.

### 4. Compare and Explain: 12 minutes

Students mark where they agreed or disagreed with the tool.

For each disagreement, students answer:

- What did I notice that the tool may have missed?
- What did the tool notice that I may have missed?
- Is the sentence ambiguous?
- Did my confidence match the quality of my evidence?

### 5. Pair Discussion: 8 minutes

Students compare one disagreement with a partner. Each pair chooses one sentence and prepares a short explanation of the strongest interpretation.

Require students to use evidence from the sentence, not just preference.

### 6. Whole-Class Debrief: 8 to 15 minutes

Discuss patterns students noticed.

Useful questions:

- Which sentences were easiest to classify? Why?
- Which sentences were hardest? Why?
- When were you overconfident?
- What clues helped you revise your thinking?
- What are the limits of using a sentiment tool for interpretation?

## Teacher Notes

Look for metacognitive behaviors rather than correct labels only. Strong student work should show clear evidence, appropriate confidence, willingness to revise, and awareness of ambiguity.

If the tool produces a surprising or questionable result, use it as a teaching moment. Ask students to evaluate the output instead of accepting it automatically.
